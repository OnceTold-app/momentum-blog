You've probably heard the term "revolving credit" when talking mortgages — maybe from a mate who swears by it, or a bank brochure that made it sound almost too good. But what actually is a revolving credit mortgage, and is it right for you? Like most things in the home loan world, the answer is: it depends.

Here's a plain-English guide to how revolving credit works in New Zealand, who it genuinely suits, and what to watch out for — including a note for property investors about the tax landscape.

## How a Revolving Credit Mortgage Works

A revolving credit mortgage (sometimes called a revolving home loan or flexi loan) functions less like a traditional mortgage and more like a large overdraft attached to your home. You're given a credit limit — often between $50,000 and $200,000 — and that credit sits in an account you can draw from and repay as needed.

The key feature: **interest is calculated daily on your outstanding balance**. So every dollar you park in that account reduces your balance, which reduces the interest you're charged — even if just for a day or two.

Most Kiwis who use this structure link their salary directly into the revolving credit account. The moment your pay hits, your mortgage balance drops, and you start paying less interest. You then pay bills and living costs from the same account throughout the month. As the balance rises and falls, so does the daily interest calculation.

MoneyHub NZ illustrates this well: if you have a $100,000 revolving credit at an indicative rate of around 7.50% (rates vary by lender and are subject to change), and your wages reduce the balance to $85,000 by month-end, you've saved interest on $15,000 for that period. Over a year, those savings add up.

## Who It Suits

Revolving credit isn't for everyone — and that's not a caveat, it's genuinely important. This structure works best for people who:

- **Have reliable, regular income** flowing into one account
- **Are financially disciplined** and won't be tempted to treat the available credit as spending money
- **Have some surplus cash** sitting around most of the month (the more your balance sits low, the more you save)
- **Prefer flexibility** — for example, those who want to make lump-sum repayments without penalty, or who have variable income like contractors or business owners

For these borrowers, a revolving credit portion can function as an interest-reducing tool and a liquid buffer at the same time. That dual role is genuinely useful.

Many NZ borrowers use revolving credit as **part of a split structure** — for example, $300,000 fixed for certainty and $100,000 revolving for flexibility. A mortgage adviser can help you work out what proportion makes sense for your situation.

## The Risks — Be Honest With Yourself

The downside of revolving credit is straightforward: if you keep drawing on it, your balance never shrinks. Unlike a table mortgage where principal repayment is baked in each fortnight, a revolving credit account only reduces if you're actively paying it down.

For borrowers who find it difficult to leave money sitting in an account, or who might treat available credit as a spending signal, the revolving structure can work against them. Over time, you could end up paying more in interest than you would have on a standard mortgage.

There's also the rate question. Revolving credit loans are typically floating-rate products, which means they move with market conditions. As at the time of writing, floating rates in New Zealand are generally higher than shorter-term fixed rates — though this gap shifts. The RBNZ's OCR decisions flow through to floating rates; the next review is scheduled for 27 May 2026.

## What This Means for Investors

For property investors, revolving credit can be a useful tool — particularly for managing cash flow between rental income and expenses, or for building a deposit for the next purchase.

One thing worth understanding in 2026: mortgage interest on residential investment properties returned to being **100% deductible** from 1 April 2025 (the 2026 tax year), following the Government's restoration of interest deductibility (Beehive.govt.nz; Inland Revenue). For investors using a revolving credit structure, accurate record-keeping of loan draws and repayments matters when claiming that deduction. Speak with your accountant about your specific situation — individual tax circumstances vary and this is not tax advice.

## What This Means For You

If you're considering a revolving credit mortgage, a few questions are worth sitting with first:

- **Do I regularly have money sitting in my transaction account for days or weeks?** If yes, that money could be reducing your mortgage balance instead.
- **Am I disciplined enough not to redraw?** The structure only saves you money if the balance trends downward over time.
- **Would a split structure suit me better?** It may be worth considering a portion fixed and a portion revolving — giving you rate certainty and flexibility.
- **Have I compared floating vs. shorter fixed rates?** A broker with access to multiple lenders can help you run the numbers.

## The Bottom Line

A revolving credit mortgage can be genuinely powerful — or quietly costly — depending on how you use it. For disciplined borrowers with steady income and surplus cash, it can shave real money off your interest bill. For others, it can become a slow leak.

The best mortgage structure isn't always the one with the lowest rate. It's the one that matches how you actually manage money. That's exactly the conversation worth having with a professional before you commit.
