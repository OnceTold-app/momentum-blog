If your fixed-rate mortgage is coming up for renewal, you're probably staring at a rate sheet and wondering the same thing thousands of other Kiwis are asking right now: should I fix for one year or two?

It's a question that sounds simple but involves more moving parts than most people realise. This article won't tell you what to do — that's a conversation to have with a licensed adviser who knows your full situation. What it will do is give you a solid framework so you walk into that conversation informed.

## Where Things Stand in 2026

The Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ) held the Official Cash Rate (OCR) at 2.25% in April 2026, noting that global uncertainty — particularly events in the Middle East — has materially altered the economic outlook. The RBNZ's own forecasts suggest the OCR could average around 2.38% by late 2026, implying roughly a coin-flip chance of one further adjustment before year end (source: RBNZ, April 2026; Opes Partners analysis, April 2026).

As of late April 2026, the average 1-year fixed mortgage rate from major NZ banks sits around **4.91%**, with floating rates averaging around **5.66%** (source: Opes Partners, 24 April 2026). Two-year rates are broadly similar, though the spread between terms varies by lender. These are indicative figures — actual rates depend on your lender, loan-to-value ratio, and any specials on offer. Rates are subject to change.

Nobody — not the banks, not the RBNZ, not the commentators — knows with certainty where rates will be in 12 or 24 months. Global events can shift the picture quickly.

## The Core Trade-off

Fixing for **one year** means you'll be back at the table sooner. If rates fall further, you'll be able to capture those lower rates relatively quickly. If rates rise, you'll have had less time locked in before you're exposed.

Fixing for **two years** gives you more certainty. You know exactly what your repayments will be for a longer stretch, which makes budgeting easier. The flip side: if rates drop meaningfully in year two, you're stuck — and breaking a fixed mortgage early can trigger significant break costs.

Neither is inherently better. The right choice depends on your personal circumstances.

## Key Factors to Weigh Up

### 1. Your Cashflow Needs

How tight is your household budget? If your mortgage repayment is a large share of your income, certainty might be worth more to you than flexibility. Locking in two years means you can plan around a known number. If you have more breathing room, you might be comfortable taking the shorter-term uncertainty.

### 2. Life Events on the Horizon

Are you expecting a significant change in the next 12–24 months? A job change, a new baby, a potential property sale or purchase, an inheritance? Fixed mortgages can be broken early, but break costs can be substantial — sometimes thousands of dollars. If there's a meaningful chance you'll need to sell or restructure your loan in the next two years, a shorter term gives you more flexibility.

### 3. The Rate Outlook — and Its Limits

The RBNZ's neutral OCR is estimated at around 3.00%, and we're currently below that — technically in stimulatory territory. Some commentators expect moderate stability through 2026, with the possibility of upward movement if inflation stays near the top of the 1–3% target band. Others point to global headwinds keeping rates contained.

The honest truth: the range of credible forecasts is wide. Economists consistently get rate predictions wrong at the 18–24 month horizon. Don't make a major financial decision based on a single prediction.

### 4. Break Costs and the Fine Print

If you fix for two years and need to break early, your bank will calculate a break cost based on the difference between your contracted rate and current wholesale rates at the time. In a falling-rate environment, break costs can be significant. Ask your bank or adviser to explain how break costs are calculated before you commit to a longer term.

### 5. Splitting Your Loan

Many Kiwis don't realise you don't have to choose a single term for your entire mortgage. Splitting your loan — say, half on a 1-year fix and half on a 2-year fix — is a legitimate strategy that gives you some flexibility while maintaining some certainty. It also means you're not rolling your entire balance at once, which reduces timing risk.

### 6. What the Rate Difference Actually Means for You

Even if there's a noticeable difference between 1-year and 2-year rates, what does that actually cost or save over your loan balance? Run the numbers. A 0.10% difference on a $500,000 mortgage is about $500 per year — meaningful, but perhaps not the deciding factor if certainty is valuable to you.

## What to Discuss With Your Adviser

When you sit down with a mortgage adviser, come prepared with answers to these questions:

- What are my plans for this property in the next 2 years?
- How would I handle repayments if rates increased by 1% at rollover?
- Am I prioritising certainty or flexibility right now?
- Is splitting my loan worth considering given my situation?

A good adviser will help you stress-test your decision against a range of rate scenarios — not just the most likely one.

## The Bottom Line

There's no universally correct answer to the 1-year vs 2-year question. The right choice is the one that fits your financial position, your risk tolerance, and your life plans — not the one that sounds best at a dinner party or appears in a headline.

Use this framework as a starting point. Then get personalised advice.
