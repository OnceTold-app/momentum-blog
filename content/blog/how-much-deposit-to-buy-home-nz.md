One of the most common questions first home buyers ask is: how much deposit do I actually need to buy a home in New Zealand? You've probably heard "20 percent" tossed around at BBQs, but the full picture is a bit more nuanced — and for many Kiwis, getting on the property ladder with less than 20% is very much possible.

Here's what you need to know.

## The 20% Rule — and Where It Comes From

The Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ) sets rules called Loan-to-Value Ratio (LVR) restrictions. These act as a "speed limit" on how much high-deposit-risk lending banks can do. In practice, most banks in New Zealand require a minimum of 20% deposit for an existing home if you're an owner-occupier (RBNZ, 2026).

That means if you're looking at a $750,000 home, you'd typically need $150,000 saved — a number that can feel out of reach, especially in the current market.

But it's not the only path.

## The 10% Loophole — New Builds and Bank Discretion

Banks are permitted to lend up to 10% of their residential mortgage book to owner-occupiers with less than 20% deposit (Opes Partners, January 2026). In practice, this allowance is most commonly applied to first home buyers.

There's also a more generous rule for **new builds**: depending on the lender, owner-occupiers may be able to purchase a newly constructed home with as little as 10% deposit. If you're open to buying new rather than existing, this can meaningfully change your savings target.

One thing to keep in mind: loans above 80% LVR (i.e., with less than 20% deposit) are considered higher-risk by lenders. Some banks apply what's known as a low equity premium — essentially a slightly higher interest rate, or additional fees — to offset that risk. It may be worth discussing with an adviser whether this affects the numbers in your situation.

## The Kāinga Ora First Home Loan: Buying with 5%

If you're a first home buyer, the government-backed **Kāinga Ora First Home Loan** is worth knowing about. Administered through participating lenders (including several main banks), it allows eligible buyers to purchase with as little as **5% deposit** — bypassing the usual LVR threshold (Kāinga Ora, 2026).

To be eligible, you'll generally need to meet criteria including:

- Purchasing within regional house price caps set by Kāinga Ora
- Earning under the income cap (currently $95,000 for a single buyer or $150,000 for two or more buyers, as at the time of writing — confirm current figures with your lender or kāingaora.govt.nz)
- Intending to live in the property

It's a meaningful option for buyers who have a steady income but haven't yet reached a 20% deposit. Eligibility criteria and caps are subject to change, so it's always worth checking the current rules directly.

## Using KiwiSaver Towards Your Deposit

If you've been contributing to KiwiSaver, that balance could form part — or all — of your deposit. Under the **KiwiSaver first home withdrawal** scheme, eligible members can withdraw most of their KiwiSaver savings to put towards a first home (Kāinga Ora / IRD).

Key eligibility points (as at May 2026):

- You must have been contributing to KiwiSaver for **at least three years**
- You must intend to live in the property
- If you've owned property before, you may still qualify under certain "second chance" rules (e.g., if you no longer have an ownership interest in any property)

One thing to note heading into 2026: the minimum KiwiSaver contribution rate increased from 3% to 3.5% on 1 April 2026 (First Home Buyers Club NZ, March 2026). If your contributions were previously set at 3%, your account balance may be growing faster than you expect — worth checking in on.

You can't withdraw the full balance — a minimum of $1,000 must remain in your account — but for many buyers, KiwiSaver forms a substantial chunk of their deposit.

## What About Property Investors?

The LVR rules are stricter for investment properties. Investors generally need a **30% deposit for existing homes** and around **20% for new builds** (Opes Partners, January 2026). This article is primarily focused on owner-occupiers, but it's worth knowing the distinction if you're buying your first home partly with investment in mind.

## What This Means For You

Here are a few things that may be worth considering as you think through your deposit:

1. **Know your target number, not just the percentage.** Work out the likely purchase price range you're aiming for, then calculate what 5%, 10%, and 20% actually looks like in dollar terms. This can make savings goals more tangible.

2. **Check your KiwiSaver balance and eligibility.** If you've been in KiwiSaver for three or more years, you may have a meaningful sum available. Your KiwiSaver provider can confirm your withdrawal eligibility.

3. **Explore whether the Kāinga Ora First Home Loan applies to you.** If you meet the income and price caps, getting in at 5% might be possible sooner than you think. A mortgage adviser can run through whether you'd qualify.

4. **Understand the trade-off of a smaller deposit.** Buying with less than 20% is achievable for many, but it may come with a low equity premium. Weighing that cost against continuing to rent and save is a personal decision that depends heavily on your circumstances.

5. **Consider whether a new build changes the maths.** With a lower deposit threshold and some tax benefits (particularly for investors), new builds can sometimes offer a different path to ownership.

## The Bottom Line

There's no single answer to how much deposit you need — it depends on the property type, your eligibility for government-backed schemes, your KiwiSaver balance, and which lender you approach. The 20% figure is a useful benchmark, but it's far from the only route in.

For many Kiwis, the real question isn't whether they can buy — it's understanding which combination of deposit, scheme, and timing works best for their situation.
